Mean Value Theorem

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) is a fundamental result in calculus that connects the values of a function to its derivatives. It provides a formal way to understand the behavior of functions over an interval.

Statement of the Mean Value Theorem

If a function \( f \) is continuous on the closed interval \([a, b]\) and differentiable on the open interval \((a, b)\), then there exists at least one point \( c \) in \((a, b)\) such that:

\( f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \)

Geometric Interpretation

The MVT implies that there is at least one point where the tangent to the curve is parallel to the secant line connecting the endpoints \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b))\).

Example: Applying the Mean Value Theorem

Consider the function \( f(x) = x^2 \) on the interval \([1, 3]\).

Step 1: Check the conditions

  • The function \( f(x) = x^2 \) is continuous on \([1, 3]\).
  • The function \( f(x) = x^2 \) is differentiable on \((1, 3)\).

Step 2: Calculate the average rate of change

\( \frac{f(3) - f(1)}{3 - 1} = \frac{9 - 1}{2} = 4 \)

Step 3: Find \( c \) such that \( f'(c) = 4 \)

\( f'(x) = 2x \)
\( 2c = 4 \)
\( c = 2 \)

So, at \( c = 2 \), the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change over the interval \([1, 3]\).
