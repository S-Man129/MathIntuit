Understanding the Tesseract

The tesseract, also known as a 4-cube, is a four-dimensional analog of the cube. Just as a cube has six square faces, a tesseract has eight cubical cells.

Mathematical Representation

In geometry, the tesseract is defined by the following set of equations:

\[ \begin{aligned} &x_1 \in [0, 1], \\ &x_2 \in [0, 1], \\ &x_3 \in [0, 1], \\ &x_4 \in [0, 1]. \end{aligned} \]

Where \( x_1, x_2, x_3, \) and \( x_4 \) are the coordinates in four-dimensional space.

Projection into 3D

The tesseract can be projected into three-dimensional space for visualization. One common projection is the Schlegel diagram, which provides an insight into the structure of a tesseract:

\[ P(x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4) = \left(\frac{x_1}{1 - x_4}, \frac{x_2}{1 - x_4}, \frac{x_3}{1 - x_4}\right) \]

This projection helps in understanding the complex structure by mapping it to 3D coordinates.

Properties

  • Vertices: 16
  • Edges: 32
  • Faces: 24 (each a square)
  • Cells: 8 (each a cube)
